Project Objective

With the adoption of the da Vinci SP at Stanford Medicine, Dr. Holsinger's team found themselves with an ever-growing stream of high-quality 4K surgical video that was largely underutilized. ViGOR is a vision-language framework that automates operative note generation directly from surgical video.

Project Overview

The ViGOR pipeline ingests 4K surgical video from the da Vinci SP, applies image treatment and frame sampling, then classifies each segment into one of nine surgical phases using a vision encoder. Those keyframes then feed into a two-pass language model: the first pass generates per-frame clinical captions, and the second synthesizes them into a complete, structured operative note.

1 / Background & Motivation

Improving Clinical Accuracy

Operative notes written hours after procedure are subject to recall bias, omission, and compression. A system that reconstructs events directly from video produces a note anchored to visual evidence.

Reducing Administrative Burden

Documentation represents a significant and growing share of a surgeon's non-clinical time. Automating the first draft of an operative note could meaningfully reduce that burden, freeing capacity for direct patient care.

Harnessing the da Vinci SP

Robotic surgical systems such as the da Vinci SP generate immense amounts of stabilized 4K video. While this sheer volume of unstructured data is difficult to process manually, it provides the perfect, highly-controlled environment for computer vision research.

2 / Dataset & Scope

The Stanford Dataset

Initiated in collaboration with Dr. Holsinger at Stanford University, our research leverages a dataset generated directly by his clinical team. The dataset includes 75 de-identified records containing synchronized 4K surgery videos alongside human-dictated operative notes.

Scope & Surgical Target

We are strictly focused on Transoral Lateral Oropharyngectomy (TLO) procedures performed on the da Vinci SP platform at a single institution, under a single surgical team. The tradeoff is limited generalizability. Results cannot be assumed to transfer to other procedures or platforms without revalidation.

3 / Data Pipeline

Image Preprocessing

Raw endoscopic frames contain black pillarbox bars and low-contrast tissue regions that impair model performance. Frames are center-cropped to square and contrast-enhanced to improve feature visibility. Mosaic tiling was also explored as a method for presenting multiple frames within a single model input.

Sampling

We explored arranging multiple frames into a single mosaic. From our experiments, we found this gave the model stronger contextual awareness across time, while also keeping costs low and run time virtually unchanged. To construct the mosaics, we used SSIM to determine the most visually distinct frames within every 20-second window.

Phase Classification

TLO is a procedure Dr. Holsinger himself teaches in nine distinct phases. This makes phase classification a natural and clinically grounded segmentation target, defined by surgical reality rather than imposed artificially. The phase labels do more than organize the video. Later we discuss how the phase labels imbue the visual embeddings with temporal metadata that guides the model through the procedure in the correct sequence. The result is a synthesized note that reads as a coherent, flowing clinical narrative rather than a disconnected series of observations.

4 / Vision-Language Architecture

Note generation follows a two-pass approach designed around the practical constraints of feeding surgical video to a language model.

Pass 1

Each sampled keyframe is captioned individually. The model receives one frame at a time with a structured clinical prompt, producing a dense, timestamped description of the instruments, anatomy, and actions visible in that moment.

Pass 2

All captions are assembled into a single chronological document and submitted in one request — text only. The model synthesizes them into a fully structured operative note organized by surgical phase, written in clinical past tense, covering procedure, findings, and hemostasis.

The two-pass design serves two purposes: it avoids content filter issues that arise when submitting raw medical imagery in bulk, and it reinforces sequential surgical context by building the narrative frame by frame before collapsing it into a final document.
